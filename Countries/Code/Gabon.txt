<p><b>Gabon</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/</a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="'g' in 'guy'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ɡ</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="/ə/ 'a' in 'about'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ə</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ˈ</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="'b' in 'buy'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">b</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="/ɒ/ short 'o' in 'body'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">ɒ</span></a></span><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English#Key" title="Help:IPA for English"><span title="'n' in 'nigh'" style="border-bottom:1px dotted">n</span></a></span><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/</a></span></span>; <small>French pronunciation:&#160;​</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[ɡabɔ̃]</a></span>), officially the <b>Gabonese Republic</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr"><i>République gabonaise</i></span>), is a <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign state</a> on the west coast of <a href="/wiki/Central_Africa" title="Central Africa">Central Africa</a>. Located on the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, Gabon is bordered by <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_Guinea" title="Equatorial Guinea">Equatorial Guinea</a> to the northwest, <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a> to the north, the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Republic of the Congo">Republic of the Congo</a> on the east and south, and the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Guinea" title="Gulf of Guinea">Gulf of Guinea</a> to the west. It has an area of nearly 270,000 square kilometres (100,000&#160;sq&#160;mi) and its population is estimated at 1.5 million people. Its capital and largest city is <a href="/wiki/Libreville" title="Libreville">Libreville</a>.</p>
<p>Since its independence from France in 1960, Gabon has had <a href="/wiki/List_of_heads_of_state_of_Gabon" title="List of heads of state of Gabon">three presidents</a>. In the early 1990s, Gabon introduced a multi-party system and a new democratic constitution that allowed for a more transparent electoral process and reformed many governmental institutions. Gabon was also a non-permanent member of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council" title="United Nations Security Council">United Nations Security Council</a> for the 2010–2011 term.</p>
<p>Low population density, abundant petroleum, and foreign private investment<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag is ambiguous, and needs clarification. (June 2012)">ambiguous</span></a></i>]</sup> have helped make Gabon one of the most prosperous countries in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>, with the 4th highest <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">HDI</a><sup id="cite_ref-HDI_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDI-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and the third highest GDP per capita (PPP) (after Equatorial Guinea and <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">Botswana</a>) in the region.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gabon's name originates from "Gabão", <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> for "cloak", which is roughly the shape of the <a href="/wiki/Estuary" title="Estuary">estuary</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Komo_River" title="Komo River">Komo River</a> by Libreville.</p>

<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Gabon" title="History of Gabon">History of Gabon</a></div>
<p>The earliest inhabitants of the area were <a href="/wiki/Congo_Pygmies" title="Congo Pygmies">Pygmy peoples</a>. They were largely replaced and absorbed by <a href="/wiki/Bantu_peoples" title="Bantu peoples">Bantu</a> tribes as <a href="/wiki/Bantu_expansion" title="Bantu expansion">they migrated</a>.</p>
<p>In the 15th century, the first Europeans arrived. By the 18th century, a <a href="/wiki/Myene_language" title="Myene language">Myeni</a> speaking kingdom known as <a href="/wiki/Orungu_Kingdom" title="Orungu Kingdom" class="mw-redirect">Orungu</a> formed in Gabon.</p>
<p>On February 10, 1722, <a href="/wiki/Bartholomew_Roberts" title="Bartholomew Roberts">Bartholomew Roberts</a>, a Welsh pirate known as Black Bart, died at sea off Cape Lopez. He raided ships off the Americas and West Africa from 1719 and 1722.</p>
<p>French explorer <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Savorgnan_de_Brazza" title="Pierre Savorgnan de Brazza">Pierre Savorgnan de Brazza</a> led his first mission to the Gabon-Congo area in 1875. He founded the town of <a href="/wiki/Franceville" title="Franceville">Franceville</a>, and was later colonial governor. Several Bantu groups lived in the area that is now Gabon when France officially occupied it in 1885.</p>
<p>In 1910 Gabon became one of the four territories of <a href="/wiki/French_Equatorial_Africa" title="French Equatorial Africa">French Equatorial Africa</a>, a federation that survived until 1959. These territories became independent on August 17, 1960. The first president of Gabon, elected in 1961, was <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on_M%27ba" title="Léon M'ba">Léon M'ba</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Omar_Bongo_Ondimba" title="Omar Bongo Ondimba" class="mw-redirect">Omar Bongo Ondimba</a> as his vice president.</p>
<p>After M'ba's accession to power, the press was suppressed, political demonstrations banned, <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_expression" title="Freedom of expression" class="mw-redirect">freedom of expression</a> curtailed, other political parties gradually excluded from power, and the Constitution changed along French lines to vest power in the Presidency, a post that M'ba assumed himself. However, when M'ba dissolved the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_(Gabon)" title="National Assembly (Gabon)" class="mw-redirect">National Assembly</a> in January 1964 to institute one-party rule, an army <a href="/wiki/Coup" title="Coup" class="mw-redirect">coup</a> sought to oust him from power and restore parliamentary democracy. French paratroopers flew in within 24 hours to restore M'ba to power.</p>
<p>After a few days of fighting, the coup ended and the opposition was imprisoned, despite widespread protests and riots. French soldiers still remain in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Camp_de_Gaulle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Camp de Gaulle (page does not exist)">Camp de Gaulle</a> on the outskirts of Gabon's capital to this day. When M'Ba died in 1967, <a href="/wiki/Bongo" title="Bongo" class="mw-disambig">Bongo</a> replaced him as president.</p>
<p>In March 1968, Bongo declared Gabon a one-party state by dissolving the BDG and establishing a new party — the <a href="/wiki/Parti_Democratique_Gabonais" title="Parti Democratique Gabonais" class="mw-redirect">Parti Democratique Gabonais</a> (PDG). He invited all Gabonese, regardless of previous political affiliation, to participate. Bongo sought to forge a single national movement in support of the government's development policies, using the PDG as a tool to submerge the regional and tribal rivalries that had divided Gabonese politics in the past. Bongo was elected President in February 1975; in April 1975, the position of vice president was abolished and replaced by the position of prime minister, who had no right to automatic succession. Bongo was re-elected President in both December 1979 and November 1986 to 7-year terms.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In early 1990 economic discontent and a desire for political liberalization provoked violent demonstrations and strikes by students and workers. In response to grievances by workers, Bongo negotiated with them on a sector-by-sector basis, making significant wage concessions. In addition, he promised to open up the PDG and to organize a national political conference in March–April 1990 to discuss Gabon's future political system. The PDG and 74 political organizations attended the conference. Participants essentially divided into two loose coalitions, the ruling PDG and its allies, and the United Front of Opposition Associations and Parties, consisting of the breakaway Morena Fundamental and the <a href="/wiki/Gabonese_Progress_Party" title="Gabonese Progress Party">Gabonese Progress Party</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The April 1990 conference approved sweeping political reforms, including creation of a national <a href="/wiki/Senate_(Gabon)" title="Senate (Gabon)" class="mw-redirect">Senate</a>, decentralization of the budgetary process, freedom of assembly and press, and cancellation of an <a href="/wiki/Exit_visa" title="Exit visa" class="mw-redirect">exit visa</a> requirement. In an attempt to guide the political system's transformation to multiparty democracy, Bongo resigned as PDG chairman and created a transitional government headed by a new Prime Minister, <a href="/wiki/Casimir_Oye-Mba" title="Casimir Oye-Mba" class="mw-redirect">Casimir Oye-Mba</a>. The Gabonese Social Democratic Grouping (RSDG), as the resulting government was called, was smaller than the previous government and included representatives from several opposition parties in its cabinet. The RSDG drafted a provisional constitution in May 1990 that provided a basic <a href="/wiki/Bill_of_rights" title="Bill of rights">bill of rights</a> and an independent judiciary but retained strong executive powers for the president. After further review by a constitutional committee and the National Assembly, this document came into force in March 1991.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Opposition to the PDG continued after the April 1990 conference, however, and in September 1990, two coup d'état attempts were uncovered and aborted. Despite anti-government demonstrations after the untimely death of an opposition leader, the first multiparty National Assembly elections in almost 30 years took place in September–October 1990, with the PDG garnering a large majority.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Following President <a href="/wiki/Omar_Bongo" title="Omar Bongo">Omar Bongo</a>'s re-election in December 1993 with 51% of the vote, opposition candidates refused to validate the election results. Serious civil disturbances led to an agreement between the government and opposition factions to work toward a political settlement. These talks led to the Paris Accords in November 1994, under which several opposition figures were included in a government of national unity. This arrangement soon broke down, however, and the 1996 and 1997 legislative and municipal elections provided the background for renewed partisan politics. The PDG won a landslide victory in the legislative election, but several major cities, including <a href="/wiki/Libreville" title="Libreville">Libreville</a>, elected opposition mayors during the 1997 local election.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Facing a divided opposition, President Omar Bongo coasted to easy re-election in December 1998, with large majorities of the vote. While Bongo's major opponents rejected the outcome as fraudulent, some international observers characterized the results as representative despite many perceived irregularities, and there were none of the civil disturbances that followed the 1993 election. Peaceful though flawed legislative elections held in 2001–2002, which were boycotted by a number of smaller opposition parties and were widely criticized for their administrative weaknesses, produced a National Assembly almost completely dominated by the PDG and allied independents. In November 2005 President Omar Bongo was elected for his sixth term. He won re-election easily, but opponents claim that the balloting process was marred by irregularities. There were some instances of violence following the announcement of his win, but Gabon generally remained peaceful.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>National Assembly elections were held again in December 2006. Several seats contested because of voting irregularities were overturned by the Constitutional Court, but the subsequent run-off elections in early 2007 again yielded a PDG-controlled National Assembly.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On June 8, 2009, President Omar Bongo died of cardiac arrest at a Spanish hospital in Barcelona, ushering in a new era in Gabonese politics. In accordance with the amended constitution, <a href="/wiki/Rose_Francine_Rogomb%C3%A9" title="Rose Francine Rogombé">Rose Francine Rogombé</a>, the President of the Senate, became Interim President on June 10, 2009. The first contested elections in Gabon's history that did not include Omar Bongo as a candidate were held on August 30, 2009 with 18 candidates for president. The lead-up to the elections saw some isolated protests, but no significant disturbances. Omar Bongo's son, ruling party leader <a href="/wiki/Ali_Bongo_Ondimba" title="Ali Bongo Ondimba">Ali Bongo Ondimba</a>, was formally declared the winner after a 3-week review by the Constitutional Court; his inauguration took place on October 16, 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The court's review had been prompted by claims of fraud by the many opposition candidates, with the initial announcement of election results sparking unprecedented violent protests in <a href="/wiki/Port-Gentil" title="Port-Gentil">Port-Gentil</a>, the country's second-largest city and a long-time bastion of opposition to PDG rule. The citizens of Port-Gentil took to the streets, and numerous shops and residences were burned, including the French Consulate and a local prison. Officially, only four deaths occurred during the riots, but opposition and local leaders claim many more. Gendarmes and the military were deployed to Port-Gentil to support the beleaguered police, and a curfew was in effect for more than 3 months.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A partial legislative by-election was held in June 2010. A newly created coalition of parties, the Union Nationale (UN), participated for the first time. The UN is composed largely of PDG defectors who left the party after <a href="/wiki/Omar_Bongo" title="Omar Bongo">Omar Bongo</a>'s death. Of the five hotly contested seats, the PDG won three and the UN won two; both sides claimed victory.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Gabon" title="Politics of Gabon">Politics of Gabon</a></div>
<p>Gabon is a republic with a presidential form of government under the 1961 constitution (revised in 1975, rewritten in 1991, and revised in 2003). The president is elected by universal suffrage for a seven-year term; a 2003 constitutional amendment removed presidential term limits and facilitated a presidency for life. The president can appoint and dismiss the prime minister, the cabinet, and judges of the independent Supreme Court. The president also has other strong powers, such as authority to dissolve the National Assembly, declare a state of siege, delay legislation, and conduct referenda.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Gabon has a bicameral legislature with a National Assembly and Senate. The National Assembly has 120 deputies who are popularly elected for a 5-year term. The Senate is composed of 102 members who are elected by municipal councils and regional assemblies and serve for 6 years. The Senate was created in the 1990–1991 constitutional revision, although it was not brought into being until after the 1997 local elections. The President of the Senate is next in succession to the President.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_culture">Political culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Political culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1990, the government made major changes to Gabon's political system. A transitional constitution was drafted in May 1990 as an outgrowth of the national political conference in March–April and later revised by a constitutional committee. Among its provisions were a Western-style bill of rights, creation of a National Council of Democracy to oversee the guarantee of those rights, a governmental advisory board on economic and social issues, and an independent judiciary.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After approval by the National Assembly, the PDG Central Committee, and the President, the Assembly unanimously adopted the constitution in March 1991. Multiparty legislative elections were held in 1990–91, despite the fact that opposition parties had not been declared formally legal. In spite of this, the elections produced the first representative, multiparty National Assembly. In January 1991, the Assembly passed by unanimous vote a law governing the legalization of opposition parties.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After President Omar Bongo was re-elected in 1993, in a disputed election where only 51% of votes were cast, social and political disturbances led to the 1994 Paris Conference and Accords. These provided a framework for the next elections. Local and legislative elections were delayed until 1996–97. In 1997, constitutional amendments put forward years earlier were adopted to create the Senate and the position of vice president, as well as to extend the president's term to seven years.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2009, newly elected President Ali Bongo Ondimba began efforts to streamline the government. In an effort to reduce corruption and government bloat, he eliminated 17 minister-level positions, abolished the vice presidency and reorganized the portfolios of numerous ministries, bureaus and directorates. In November 2009, President Bongo Ondimba announced a new vision for the modernization of Gabon, called "Gabon Emergent". This program contains three pillars: Green Gabon, Service Gabon, and Industrial Gabon. The goals of Gabon Emergent are to diversify the economy so that Gabon becomes less reliant on petroleum, to eliminate corruption, and to modernize the workforce. Under this program, exports of raw timber have been banned, a government-wide census was held, the work day has been changed to eliminate a long midday break, and a national oil company was created.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In provisional results,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (December 2012)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> the ruling <a href="/wiki/Gabonese_Democratic_Party" title="Gabonese Democratic Party">Gabonese Democratic Party</a> (PDG) won 84 out of 120 parliamentary seats.</p>
<p>On January 25, 2011, opposition leader <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Mba_Obame" title="André Mba Obame">André Mba Obame</a> claimed the presidency, saying the country should be run by someone the people really wanted. He also selected 19 ministers for his government, and the entire group, along with hundreds of others, spent the night at UN headquarters. On January 26, the government dissolved Mba Obame's party. AU chairman <a href="/wiki/Jean_Ping" title="Jean Ping">Jean Ping</a> said that Mba Obame's action "hurts the integrity of legitimate institutions and also endangers the peace, the security and the stability of Gabon."<sup id="cite_ref-Goma_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goma-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Interior Minister Jean-François Ndongou accused Mba Obame and his supporters of <a href="/wiki/Treason" title="Treason">treason</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Goma_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goma-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The UN Secretary-General, <a href="/wiki/Ban_Ki-moon" title="Ban Ki-moon">Ban Ki-moon</a>, said that he recognized Ondimba as the only official Gabonese president.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_relations">Foreign relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Foreign relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Foreign_relations_of_Gabon" title="Foreign relations of Gabon">Foreign relations of Gabon</a></div>
<p>Since independence, Gabon has followed a nonaligned policy, advocating dialogue in international affairs and recognizing each side of divided countries. In inter-African affairs, Gabon espouses development by evolution rather than revolution and favors regulated free enterprise as the system most likely to promote rapid economic growth. Gabon played an important leadership role in the stability of Central Africa through involvement in mediation efforts in <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Republic" title="Central African Republic">Central African Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Republic of the Congo">Republic of the Congo</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a> (D.R.C.), and <a href="/wiki/Burundi" title="Burundi">Burundi</a>.</p>
<p>In December 1999, through the mediation efforts of President Bongo, a peace accord was signed in the Republic of the Congo (Brazzaville) between the government and most leaders of an armed rebellion. President Bongo was also involved in the continuing D.R.C. peace process, and played a role in mediating the crisis in <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te_d%27Ivoire" title="Côte d'Ivoire" class="mw-redirect">Côte d'Ivoire</a>. Gabonese armed forces were also an integral part of the Central African Economic and Monetary Community (CEMAC) mission to the Central African Republic.</p>
<p>Gabon is a member of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> (UN) and some of its specialized and related agencies, as well as of the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a>; the <a href="/wiki/IMF" title="IMF" class="mw-redirect">IMF</a>; the <a href="/wiki/African_Union" title="African Union">African Union</a> (AU); the Central African Customs Union/Central African Economic and Monetary Community (UDEAC/CEMAC); EU/ACP association under the <a href="/wiki/Lome_Convention" title="Lome Convention" class="mw-redirect">Lome Convention</a>; the <a href="/wiki/Communaute_Financiere_Africaine" title="Communaute Financiere Africaine" class="mw-redirect">Communaute Financiere Africaine</a> (CFA); the <a href="/wiki/Organization_of_the_Islamic_Conference" title="Organization of the Islamic Conference" class="mw-redirect">Organization of the Islamic Conference</a> (OIC); the <a href="/wiki/Nonaligned_Movement" title="Nonaligned Movement" class="mw-redirect">Nonaligned Movement</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/Economic_Community_of_Central_African_States" title="Economic Community of Central African States">Economic Community of Central African States</a> (ECCAS/CEEAC), among others. In 1995, Gabon withdrew from the <a href="/wiki/Organization_of_Petroleum_Exporting_Countries" title="Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries" class="mw-redirect">Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries</a> (OPEC). Gabon was elected to a non-permanent seat on the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council" title="United Nations Security Council">United Nations Security Council</a> for January 2010 through December 2011 and held the rotating presidency in March 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Military_of_Gabon" title="Military of Gabon">Military of Gabon</a></div>
<p>Gabon has a small, professional <a href="/wiki/Military_of_Gabon" title="Military of Gabon">military</a> of about 5,000 personnel, divided into army, navy, air force, <a href="/wiki/Gendarmerie" title="Gendarmerie">gendarmerie</a>, and police. Gabonese forces are oriented to the defense of the country and have not been trained for an offensive role. A 1,800-member guard provides security for the president.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Gabon" title="Geography of Gabon">Geography of Gabon</a></div>
<p>Gabon is located on the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> coast of central Africa. Located on the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, between latitudes <a href="/wiki/3rd_parallel_north" title="3rd parallel north">3°N</a> and <a href="/wiki/4th_parallel_south" title="4th parallel south">4°S</a>, and longitudes <a href="/wiki/8th_meridian_east" title="8th meridian east">8°</a> and <a href="/wiki/15th_meridian_east" title="15th meridian east">15°E</a>. Gabon generally has an <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_climate" title="Equatorial climate" class="mw-redirect">equatorial climate</a> with an extensive system of rainforests covering 85% of the country.</p>
<p>There are three distinct regions: the coastal plains (ranging between 20 to 300&#160;km from the ocean's shore), the mountains (the Cristal Mountains to the northeast of Libreville, the Chaillu Massif in the centre, culminating at 1575 m with Mont Iboundji), and the <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">savanna</a> in the east. The coastal plains form a large section of the <a href="/wiki/World_Wildlife_Fund" title="World Wildlife Fund" class="mw-redirect">World Wildlife Fund</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Equatorial_coastal_forests" title="Atlantic Equatorial coastal forests">Atlantic Equatorial coastal forests</a> <a href="/wiki/Ecoregion" title="Ecoregion">ecoregion</a> and contain patches of <a href="/wiki/Central_African_mangroves" title="Central African mangroves">Central African mangroves</a> especially on the <a href="/wiki/Muni_River" title="Muni River">Muni River</a> estuary on the border with <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_Guinea" title="Equatorial Guinea">Equatorial Guinea</a>.</p>
<p>Gabon's largest river is the <a href="/wiki/Ogoou%C3%A9" title="Ogooué" class="mw-redirect">Ogooué</a> which is 1200&#160;km long. Gabon has three <a href="/wiki/Karst_topography" title="Karst topography" class="mw-redirect">karst</a> areas where there are hundreds of caves located in the dolomite and limestone rocks. Some of the caves include Grotte du Lastoursville, Grotte du Lebamba, Grotte du Bongolo, and Grotte du Kessipougou. Many caves have not been explored yet. A National Geographic Expedition visited the caves in the summer of 2008 to document them (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://blogs.nationalgeographic.com/blogs/gabon-caves">Expedition Website</a>).</p>
<p>Gabon is also noted for efforts to preserve the natural environment. In 2002, President Omar Bongo Ondimba put Gabon firmly on the map as an important future ecotourism destination by designating roughly 10% of the nation's territory to be part of its <a href="/wiki/List_of_national_parks_of_Gabon" title="List of national parks of Gabon">national park system</a> (with 13 parks in total), one of the largest proportions of <a href="/wiki/Nature_park" title="Nature park">nature parkland</a> in the world. The <a href="/wiki/National_Agency_for_National_Parks" title="National Agency for National Parks">National Agency for National Parks</a> manages Gabon's national park system.</p>
<p>Natural resources include: petroleum, magnesium, iron, gold, uranium, and forests.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Gabon" title="Economy of Gabon">Economy of Gabon</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gabon_Export_Treemap.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Gabon_Export_Treemap.jpg/350px-Gabon_Export_Treemap.jpg" width="350" height="273" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Gabon_Export_Treemap.jpg/525px-Gabon_Export_Treemap.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Gabon_Export_Treemap.jpg/700px-Gabon_Export_Treemap.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2670" data-file-height="2086" /></a>

</div>
</div>
<p>Gabon's economy is dominated by oil. Oil revenues comprise roughly 46% of the government's budget, 43% of gross domestic product (GDP), and 81% of exports. Oil production is now declining rapidly from its high point of 370,000 barrels per day in 1997. Some estimates suggest that Gabonese oil will be expended by 2025. In spite of the decreasing oil revenues, planning is only now beginning for an after-oil scenario.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The Grondin <a href="/wiki/Oil_field" title="Oil field">Oil Field</a> was discovered in 50&#160;m water depths 40&#160;km offshore, in 1971 and produces from the Batanga <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstones</a> of <a href="/wiki/Maastrichtian" title="Maastrichtian">Maastrichtian</a> age forming an <a href="/wiki/Anticline" title="Anticline">anticline</a> salt <a href="/wiki/Structural_trap" title="Structural trap">structural trap</a> which is about 2&#160;km deep.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Gabonese public expenditures from the years of significant oil revenues were not spent efficiently. Overspending on the Transgabonais railroad, the oil price shock of 1986, the CFA franc devaluation of 1994, and low oil prices in the late 1990s caused serious debt problems that still plague the country.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Gabon earned a poor reputation with the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Club" title="Paris Club">Paris Club</a> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a> (IMF) over the management of its debt and revenues. Successive IMF missions have criticized the government for overspending on off-budget items (in good years and bad), over-borrowing from the Central Bank, and slipping on the schedule for privatization and administrative reform. However, in September 2005, Gabon successfully concluded a 15-month Stand-By Arrangement with the IMF. Another 3-year Stand-By Arrangement with the IMF was approved in May 2007. Because of the financial crisis and social developments surrounding the death of President Omar Bongo and the elections, Gabon was unable to meet its economic goals under the Stand-By Arrangement in 2009. Negotiations with the IMF are ongoing.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Gabon's oil revenues have given it a strong per capita GDP of $8,600, extremely high for the region. However the income distribution is skewed and social indicators show poor values. The richest 20% of the population earn over 90% of the income while about a third of the Gabonese population lives in poverty.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The economy is highly dependent on extraction of abundant primary materials. Prior to the discovery of oil, logging was the pillar of the Gabonese economy. Today, logging and manganese mining are the other major income generators. Recent explorations point to the presence of the world's largest unexploited iron ore deposit. For many living in the countryside without access to employment in extractive industries, remittances from family members in urban areas or subsistence activities provide income.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Many foreign and local observers have consistently lamented the lack of diversity in the Gabonese economy. Various factors have so far stymied additional industries—a small market of about 1 million people, dependence on French imports, inability to capitalize on regional markets, lack of entrepreneurial zeal among the Gabonese, and the fairly regular stream of oil "rent". Further investment in agricultural or <a href="/wiki/Tourism_in_Gabon" title="Tourism in Gabon">tourism</a> sectors is complicated by poor infrastructure. The small processing and service sectors that do exist are largely dominated by a few prominent local investors.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>At World Bank and IMF insistence, the government embarked in the 1990s on a program of privatization of its state-owned companies and administrative reform, including reducing public sector employment and salary growth, but progress has been slow. The new government has voiced a commitment to work toward an economic transformation of the country but faces significant challenges to realize this goal.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society">Society</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Society">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_Gabon" title="Demographics of Gabon">Demographics of Gabon</a></div>
<p>Gabon has a population that is estimated at 1,545,255.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Historical and environmental factors caused Gabon's population to decline between 1900 and 1940.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Gabon has one of the lowest <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population densities</a> of any country in Africa,<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> and the fourth highest <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">Human Development Index</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HDI_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDI-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethnic_groups">Ethnic groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Ethnic groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Almost all Gabonese are of <a href="/wiki/Bantu_peoples" title="Bantu peoples">Bantu</a> origin. Gabon has at least forty <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnic groups</a> with differing languages and cultures.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Beti-Pahuin#Fang" title="Beti-Pahuin" class="mw-redirect">Fang</a> are generally thought to be the largest,<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> although recent census data seem to favor the Nzebi.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Others include the <a href="/wiki/Myene_language" title="Myene language">Myene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kota_people" title="Kota people">Kota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shira_people" title="Shira people">Shira</a>, <a href="/wiki/Punu_people" title="Punu people">Puru</a>, and Kande.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Ethnic boundaries are less sharply drawn in Gabon than elsewhere in <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>. There are also various <a href="/wiki/Pygmy" title="Pygmy" class="mw-redirect">Pygmy</a> peoples: the <a href="/wiki/Bongo_people_(Gabon)" title="Bongo people (Gabon)">Bongo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kota_people" title="Kota people">Kota</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Baka_people_(Cameroon_and_Gabon)" title="Baka people (Cameroon and Gabon)">Baka</a>; the latter speak the only non-Bantu language in Gabon.</p>
<p>Most ethnicities are spread throughout Gabon, leading to constant contact and interaction among the groups. Intermarriage between the ethnicities is quite common, helping reduce ethnic tensions. French, the language of its former colonial ruler, is a unifying force. The Democratic Party of Gabon (PDG)'s historical dominance also has served to unite various ethnicities and local interests into a larger whole. More than 10,000 native French live in Gabon, including an estimated 2,000 dual nationals.<sup id="cite_ref-bn_4-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bn-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Languages">Languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Gabon" title="Languages of Gabon">Languages of Gabon</a></div>
<p>It is <a href="/wiki/African_French#note-2007_report" title="African French">estimated that 80%</a> of Gabon's population can speak French, and that 30% of Libreville residents are native speakers of the language. Nationally, 32% of the Gabonese people speak the <a href="/wiki/Fang_language" title="Fang language">Fang language</a> as a mother tongue.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In October 2012, just before the 14th summit of the <a href="/wiki/Organisation_internationale_de_la_Francophonie" title="Organisation internationale de la Francophonie">Organisation internationale de la Francophonie</a>, the country declared an intention to add English as a second official language, reportedly in response to an investigation by France into corruption in the African country,<sup id="cite_ref-independent_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> though a government spokesman insisted it was for practical reasons only.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_Gabon" title="Religion in Gabon">Religion in Gabon</a></div>
<p>Major religions practiced in Gabon include <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> (<a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" title="Roman Catholicism" class="mw-redirect">Roman Catholicism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestantism</a>), <a href="/wiki/Bwiti" title="Bwiti">Bwiti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, and indigenous <a href="/wiki/Animism" title="Animism">animistic</a> religion.<sup id="cite_ref-report_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-report-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Many persons practice elements of both Christianity and traditional indigenous religious beliefs.<sup id="cite_ref-report_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-report-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Approximately 73 percent of the population, including noncitizens, practice at least some elements of Christianity, including the syncretistic Bwiti; 12 percent practice Islam (of whom 80 to 90 percent are foreigners); 10 percent practice traditional indigenous religious beliefs exclusively; and 5 percent practice no religion or are <a href="/wiki/Atheists" title="Atheists" class="mw-redirect">atheists</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-report_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-report-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Gabon's literacy rate is 88.4%.<sup id="cite_ref-cinnwk_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cinnwk-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Health_in_Gabon" title="Health in Gabon">Health in Gabon</a></div>
<p>Most of the health services of Gabon are public, but there are some private institutions, of which the best known is the hospital established in 1913 in <a href="/wiki/Lambar%C3%A9n%C3%A9" title="Lambaréné">Lambaréné</a> by <a href="/wiki/Albert_Schweitzer" title="Albert Schweitzer">Albert Schweitzer</a>. Gabon's medical infrastructure is considered one of the best in <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a>. By 1985 there were 28 hospitals, 87 medical centers, and 312 infirmaries and dispensaries. As of 2004, there were an estimated 29 physicians per 100,000 people. Approximately 90% of the population had access to health care services.</p>
<p>In 2000, 70% of the population had access to safe drinking water and 21% had adequate sanitation. A comprehensive government health program treats such diseases as <a href="/wiki/Leprosy" title="Leprosy">leprosy</a>, <a href="/wiki/African_trypanosomiasis" title="African trypanosomiasis">sleeping sickness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Filariasis" title="Filariasis">filariasis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Intestinal_worms" title="Intestinal worms" class="mw-redirect">intestinal worms</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a>. Rates for immunization of children under the age of one were 97% for tuberculosis and 65% for <a href="/wiki/Polio" title="Polio" class="mw-redirect">polio</a>. Immunization rates for <a href="/wiki/DPT_vaccine" title="DPT vaccine">DPT</a> and <a href="/wiki/Measles" title="Measles">measles</a> were 37% and 56% respectively. Gabon has a domestic supply of pharmaceuticals from a large, modern factory in Libreville.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Total_fertility_rate" title="Total fertility rate">total fertility rate</a> has decreased from 5.8 in 1960 to 4.2 children per mother during childbearing years in 2000. Ten percent of all births were low birth weight. The <a href="/wiki/Maternal_mortality_rate" title="Maternal mortality rate" class="mw-redirect">maternal mortality rate</a> was 520 per 100,000 live births as of 1998. In 2005, the <a href="/wiki/Infant_mortality_rate" title="Infant mortality rate" class="mw-redirect">infant mortality rate</a> was 55.35 per 1,000 live births and life expectancy was 55.02 years. As of 2002, the overall mortality rate was estimated at 17.6 per 1,000 inhabitants.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a> <a href="/wiki/Prevalence" title="Prevalence">prevalence</a> is estimated to be 5.2% of the adult population (ages 15–49).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> As of 2009, approximately 46,000 people were living with HIV/AIDS.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> There were an estimated 2,400 deaths from AIDS in 2009 – down from 3,000 deaths in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Gabon" title="Education in Gabon">Education in Gabon</a></div>
<p>Gabon's education system is regulated by two ministries: The Ministry of Education, in charge of pre-kindergarten through the last High School Grade, and the Ministry of Higher Education and Innovative Technologies, in charge of Universities and Higher Education and Professional Schools.</p>
<p>Education is compulsory for children ages 6 to 16 under the Education Act. Most children in Gabon start their school lives by attending Nurseries or "Crèche", then Kindergarten known as "Jardins d'Enfants". At age 6, they are enrolled in Primary School, "École Primaire" which is made up of six grades. The next level is "École Secondaire", which is made up of seven grades. The planned graduation age is 19 years old. Those who graduate can apply for admission at institutions of Higher learning, including engineering schools or business schools.</p>
<p>The government has used oil revenue for school construction, paying teachers' salaries, and promoting education, including in rural areas. However, maintenance of school structures, as well as teachers' salaries, has been declining. In 2002 the gross primary enrollment rate was 132 percent, and in 2000 the net primary enrollment rate was 78 percent. Gross and net enrollment ratios are based on the number of students formally registered in primary school and therefore do not necessarily reflect actual school attendance. As of 2001, 69 percent of children who started primary school were likely to reach grade 5. Problems in the education system include poor management and planning, lack of oversight, poorly qualified teachers, and overcrowded classrooms.<sup id="cite_ref-ilab_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ilab-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Gabon" title="Culture of Gabon">Culture of Gabon</a></div>
<p>A country with a primarily <a href="/wiki/Oral_tradition" title="Oral tradition">oral tradition</a> up until the spread of literacy in the 21st century, Gabon is rich in <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">mythology</a>. "Raconteurs" are currently working to keep traditions alive such as the mvett among the Fangs and the ingwala among the Nzebis.</p>
<p>Gabon also features internationally celebrated masks, such as the n'goltang (Fang) and the relicary figures of the <a href="/wiki/Kota_people" title="Kota people">Kota</a>. Each group has its own set of masks used for various reasons. They are mostly used in traditional ceremonies such as marriage, birth and funerals. Traditionalists mainly work with rare local woods and other precious materials.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Gabon" title="Music of Gabon">Music of Gabon</a></div>
<p>Gabonese music is lesser-known in comparison with regional giants like the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cameroon" title="Cameroon">Cameroon</a>. The country boasts an array of folk styles, as well as pop stars like <a href="/wiki/Patience_Dabany" title="Patience Dabany">Patience Dabany</a> and Annie Flore Batchiellilys, a Gabonese singer and renowned live performer. Also known are guitarists like Georges Oyendze, La Rose Mbadou and Sylvain Avara, and the singer <a href="/wiki/Oliver_N%27Goma" title="Oliver N'Goma">Oliver N'Goma</a>.</p>
<p>Imported rock and hip hop from the US and UK are popular in Gabon, as are <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Rumba" title="Cuban Rumba" class="mw-redirect">rumba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Makossa" title="Makossa">makossa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soukous" title="Soukous">soukous</a>. Gabonese folk instruments include the obala, the ngombi, <a href="/wiki/Balafon" title="Balafon">balafon</a> and traditional drums.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gabon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Media_of_Gabon" title="Media of Gabon">Media of Gabon</a></div>
<p>Radio-Diffusion Télévision Gabonaise (RTG), which is owned and operated by the government, broadcasts in <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> and indigenous languages. Color television broadcasts have been introduced in major cities. In 1981, a commercial radio station, Africa No. 1, began operations. The most powerful radio station on the continent, it has participation from the French and Gabonese governments and private European media.</p>
<p>In 2004, the government operated two radio stations and another seven were privately owned. There were also two government television stations and four privately owned. In 2003, there were an estimated 488 radios and 308 television sets for every 1,000 people. About 11.5 of every 1,000 people were cable subscribers. Also in 2003, there were 22.4 personal computers for every 1,000 people and 26 of every 1,000 people had access to the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>. The national press service is the Gabonese Press Agency, which publishes a daily paper, Gabon-Matin (circulation 18,000 as of 2002).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/L%27Union" title="L'Union">L'Union</a> in Libreville, the government-controlled daily newspaper, had an average daily circulation of 40,000 in 2002. The weekly Gabon d'Aujourdhui, is published by the Ministry of Communications. There are about nine privately owned periodicals which are either independent or affiliated with political parties. These publish in small numbers and are often delayed by financial constraints. The constitution of Gabon provides for free speech and a free press, and the government supports these rights. Several periodicals actively criticize the government and foreign publications are widely available.</p>
